Wait But Why, Simplified

The article in 3 minutes, then in depth

The core argument in the Wait But Why article is that technological change may feel slow for a while, then become extremely fast once AI systems become more general and self-improving. The practical implication is simple: prepare institutions now.

Stage 1

ANI (today)

  • AI does specific tasks only.
  • Examples: translation, diagnostics, recommendations, customer support.
  • Powerful but narrow.
Stage 2

AGI (future)

  • AI can do many cognitive tasks like a human.
  • Can reason across domains, not just one workflow.
  • Still uncertain when exactly this arrives.
Stage 3

ASI (hypothetical)

  • AI exceeds human capability by a very large margin.
  • Main concern is control, alignment, and governance.
  • Could be hugely beneficial or highly risky.

Core logic of the article

More compute
Better models
General capability
Self-improvement loops
Very fast transition risk

Practical takeaway: do not wait for certainty. Build capability, governance, and social safeguards in parallel.

What the article gets right

  • People often underestimate exponential improvement in technical systems.
  • Even narrow AI can reshape institutions before AGI arrives.
  • Governance usually lags technology unless intentional preparation begins early.
  • The transition is not only technical; it is civilizational.

What professionals should adapt for India

  • Do not assume uniform digital readiness across districts and communities.
  • Design for assisted access: voice, local language, and human facilitation.
  • Treat AI as public infrastructure when used in welfare, justice, health, and livelihoods.
  • Track inclusion outcomes, not only productivity outcomes.

In-Depth Analysis

How AI can reshape society, economy, and state capacity

AI’s impact is not one single story. It creates gains and risks simultaneously. For professionals, the right question is: which systems improve, who benefits, who is excluded, and what safeguards are active.

Labor Markets

Routine cognitive work will automate first; demand rises for roles involving judgment, trust, field coordination, and interdisciplinary design.

Public Services

Potential gains in grievance redressal, eligibility screening, document flow, and case triage, if accountability remains human-led.

Knowledge Access

High-quality guidance may become cheap and scalable through multilingual assistants, reducing expert scarcity in remote areas.

Power Structures

Without governance, AI can centralize control and bias. With governance, it can democratize insight and reduce rent-seeking bottlenecks.

Professional Practice

How professionals can exercise AI for common good

The goal is not “AI adoption” in abstraction. The goal is measurable public value with safeguards. Use this as a practical operating protocol.

1. Start with a public problem

Pick one concrete pain point: delayed pensions, poor grievance closure, crop advisory mismatch, school learning support, etc.

2. Define the metric before the model

Set baseline and target first: time-to-service, error rate, inclusion gap, trust score, cost per case.

3. Keep a human accountable

AI can recommend; institutions decide. No high-stakes decision should be fully unreviewed.

4. Design for the last mile

Assume low bandwidth, multilingual needs, low digital fluency, and assisted usage scenarios.

5. Publish model limits

State what the system can do, cannot do, and when to escalate to human experts.

6. Institutionalize audits

Run bias audits, failure reviews, and grievance analytics on fixed intervals.

Professional accountability checklist

  • Is the problem definition socially grounded?
  • Is there a fallback path when AI fails?
  • Are affected communities represented in design?
  • Is there a documented appeals mechanism?
  • Are outcomes published in understandable language?

Leadership stance for IRMAns

  • Resist overclaiming and under-measuring.
  • Make evidence public, including failed pilots.
  • Train teams in ethics, operations, and social context together.
  • Build coalitions: state, civil society, academia, market actors.
  • Protect dignity and rights as non-negotiable design principles.

Use these answers as operating hypotheses. In practice, always localize by district context and validate with field data.
1. Is this vision too ambitious for India right now?

Ambition is acceptable; vague claims are not. Treat every claim as testable and publish proof by milestones.

2. Can AI by itself solve communal tension and social polarization?

No. AI can improve fairness in delivery systems, but social conflict requires political, legal, and cultural work.

3. If literacy is uneven, how can AI be inclusive?

Use voice interfaces, local languages, assisted access points, and community mediators. Inclusion must be designed, not assumed.

4. Will AI mostly eliminate jobs?

Some tasks will disappear, many jobs will reconfigure. New demand rises for coordination, judgment, care, and field integration.

5. How should NGOs responsibly adopt AI?

Start with one workflow, define success metrics, train staff, maintain human review, and create a grievance channel.

6. What is the minimum proof standard for a public-interest AI pilot?

Baseline data, intervention log, outcome metrics, error analysis, inclusion impact, and independent review summary.

7. How do we prevent AI from becoming only a marketing narrative?

Require monthly evidence dashboards and independent validation. Reward outcomes, not announcements.

8. Should we wait for AGI before acting?

No. Most near-term public value comes from current AI systems if implemented responsibly.

9. How do we handle bias in models serving vulnerable communities?

Use representative data, periodic fairness audits, community review, and clear appeal pathways.

10. Can AI help reduce corruption in service delivery?

It can reduce process opacity through audit trails and anomaly detection, but strong institutional enforcement is still required.

11. What should district administrators ask vendors before deployment?

Ask about data governance, model limits, human override, auditability, security controls, and accountability in case of harm.

12. How can cooperatives and FPOs use AI meaningfully?

Demand forecasting, procurement planning, input optimization, price advisory, and multilingual member communication.

13. Is open-source AI always better for social impact?

Not always. Choose based on capability, safety, cost, localization needs, and long-term maintainability.

14. How should professionals balance speed and safety?

Use phased deployment: sandbox, controlled pilot, monitored scale-up, then institutional adoption.

15. What metrics matter beyond efficiency?

Trust scores, equity impact, grievance resolution quality, and user dignity indicators matter as much as speed/cost.

16. What would real success look like in 2 years?

Replicable district models, measurable inclusion gains, lower service delays, and durable institutional ownership.

17. What mistakes should we avoid in the first 6 months?

Over-scoping, poor data hygiene, ignoring frontline users, and deploying without escalation protocols.

18. How can IRMA alumni collaborate at scale?

Form domain cohorts, share common templates, publish open evidence, and mentor state/NGO implementation teams.

Execution

An expanded action roadmap for IRMA alumni

Month 0-1

Constitute domain squads

Set up squads for agriculture, livelihoods, health, local governance, and justice access.

Month 2-4

Deploy measured pilots

Launch one rural and one urban-poor pilot per domain with clear baseline metrics.

Month 5-8

Publish proof dashboards

Release monthly scorecards with successes, failures, and corrective actions.

Month 9-24

Institutionalize and scale

Integrate proven workflows into NGO/state operations with training and governance playbooks.

Operating rule: no claim without data, no scale without inclusion, no automation without accountability.
